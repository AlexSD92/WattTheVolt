Balancing Study, Family, and Sunshine - Part 2

Flying Out to Spain – 27-07-25

Two and a half hours doesn’t sound like a lot of time, but when you’re sitting next to strangers on a cramped aircraft going 500+ mph, you’re better off finding something to keep your mind occupied. Jokes aside, the flight isn’t the worst part—it’s the build-up, in my opinion.

It was a later flight (after my kids’ bedtime), so we had all day to carry on as normal. I went to the gym, we went out for lunch, sorted school cardigans for the girls, tidied the house, watched some TV, had dinner—you get the picture. But at some point, you have to shift gears. Every family has a different threshold for this.

Some families leave it to the last half hour: pack, taxi, go. Not us. Our prep started weeks ago—mostly for my partner’s benefit (she’s the planner). Personally, I’m happy with a 3–5 hour packing window. Any more and I stress. Any less and I panic.

As I’m writing this, I’m thinking about how much I want to land. But then comes the *wind-down*—that painfully slow shuffle off the plane. And there’s always someone who takes too long. Not the elderly or families with kids—they’re legends. I mean the people who could’ve moved faster if they hadn’t packed 30 minutes before their taxi arrived. (No offence… but also, kind of offence.)

I’ll leave this note here. I’ve got a window seat, and we’re cruising alongside the sunset. It’s a killer view. I’m taking a few minutes to appreciate the moment. After millions of years of evolution and progress, I get to be here—flying through the sky with coffee in my system and my family by my side. Wild, isn’t it?

Summary of Week 1 – 04-08-25

I set myself the goal of completing five lectures this week, giving myself a couple of days off to relax. I nearly hit the mark—I completed four. Technically, I could have squeezed in one more, but the lecturer in the last session suggested reviewing the material before the next phase. That felt like a natural pause, and honestly, I needed a moment to digest everything. It’s been a firehose of information.

As for the balance between study and family—surprisingly, it worked out well. That’s largely thanks to my partner, who’s incredibly supportive of everything I do. Here's what my days have looked like:

  1. Wake up between 08:00 and 10:00. Breakfast and a strong percolator coffee—arguably the best kind of coffee.
  2. Watch the first half of the 3-hour lecture. The lecturer always inserts a 10-minute break midway, which makes for a natural stopping point.
  3. Get everyone ready for the beach or pool. Sunscreen, snacks, toys, floaties—the works. We’d usually be out by 11:00–12:00, back by 14:00 for lunch prep.
  4. Eat around 15:00. Clean up by 16:00.
  5. Second coffee and second half of the lecture while the girls relaxed with their tablets.
  6. Evening swim or beach time until 20:00. Then back upstairs, dinner for the kids, and bedtime routine.
  7. After they were down, we’d either chill on the terrace or sneak out for some local food while my parents stayed with the kids.

What’s been great is the ability to watch lectures at 2× speed. The lecturer talks slowly to help note-takers, which I appreciate, but it’s too slow for me. With occasional pauses for complex topics, I get through them in about 90 minutes.

So far, I’d absolutely recommend this learning style for anyone with a busy life. It’s flexible and designed to fit around your schedule. I know the practical modules will be more rigid, but the weekend options for full-time workers are a real bonus.
