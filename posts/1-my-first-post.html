My First Post

This is my first post here. I'm not really too sure what to say. This blog is kind of an impulse decision—but at the same time, it’s not. Honestly, I’ve made a couple of impulsive decisions in the last month or so. The first was buying a new car. I’ve been driving around a horrendous beater for the last 10 years, and I was finally due an upgrade.

It was a 2007 Suzuki Liana that left me stranded more than once on motorways and dual carriageways. The final straw came in February this year—I was driving home from work when I heard something metallic hit the road. I looked in the rear-view mirror and saw my tailpipe rolling away behind me like it was trying to make a run for it. That was it. Time for something that wouldn’t embarrass me at roundabouts.

I then registered for training to become an electrician. And finally, I decided to start this blog—not just to document my journey, but to share what I learn. Hopefully, once I’m qualified, I’ll use it as a springboard for starting my own business.

This isn’t the first time I’ve jumped into something like this. Not long ago, I signed up for a coding bootcamp out of curiosity. The goal was to get sick at coding, go self-employed, and crush it—but it didn’t quite work out that way. While I loved what I learned, I didn’t really feel that the bootcamp prepared me for actual employment.

I didn’t leave with one standout project I was proud of—but I left with skills I genuinely value. The hardest part? Getting the front end to talk to the back end. I was using React with Django REST, and I spent hours trying to debug why my cookie-based logins weren’t working. Tutors checked my code, everything looked fine. In the end, I discovered I was in incognito mode the whole time—so no cookies were being saved. Rookie mistake, but a lesson I’ll never forget.

If you want to hear more about that journey, fill out the contact form—I’ll even give you a shoutout. Despite the outcome, I still love coding, and the skills I picked up will stick with me for life. And yes—if you're tech-savvy, you’ll notice this is a simple, static blog. That’s intentional. I wanted full control and minimal cost.

Anyway, here I am, trying another bootcamp. It wasn’t as impulsive as I might’ve made it seem. I did some research—mostly talking to my dad (he’s got a lot of electrician friends), watching "day in the life" videos, and reading up on industry trends and future demand.

Part of it came from a craving to work with my hands again—not just sit at a desk all day. The bits of my current job I actually enjoy are when I’m out and about, being physical. Plus, I’ve always been a bit of a nerd when it comes to physics and learning how things work. It wasn’t one big moment that pushed me over the edge—more like a slow, dawning realisation: I am so tired of being bored in this office.

I used to think a desk job was the dream—work-life balance, steady 9–5, consistent salary. But the older I get, the more I realise something’s missing. It’s not about money anymore—it’s about freedom. You can always earn more money, but you can’t earn more time.

For me, freedom means making enough money to live well, while actually having the time to enjoy it. I don’t mind working solo, but there’s nothing better than being part of a tight-knit team that clicks. My ideal workday? Wake up early, get on site, crack on with the job, quick lunch, then back at it until the work’s done. Straightforward, physical, satisfying.

I think that about wraps it up for now. I just wanted to introduce myself and talk about why I’m doing this. In the future, I plan to post resources for other students, maybe share ideas around starting a business. But mostly, I want this blog to be a space to reflect and document the journey.

There’s already so much content out there about “how to learn to code” or “how to break into tech,” but most of it feels like it’s selling a dream rather than teaching a craft. I noticed a lot of it is just recycled advice—people trying to position themselves as mentors without even having job experience. It’s easy to fall into the trap of chasing income instead of skill. I want this blog to be the opposite of that—honest, grounded, and real.
