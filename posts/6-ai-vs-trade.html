Will Electricians Triumph in the AI Race?

The other day, I saw a quote floating around from Jensen Huang, CEO of Nvidia—the company behind most of the world’s high-performance AI chips. In an interview with Channel 4 News, he reportedly said:

“Electricians and plumbers will be the big winners in the AI race.”

I haven’t been able to load the full interview yet (the Channel 4 link was broken at the time), but that quote has popped up in multiple credible places—LinkedIn, X (formerly Twitter), and a few blogs. Whether he said it off-the-cuff or with a bit of weight behind it, I think the quote hits on something real.

Why would tradespeople 'win' in the AI race?

On the surface, it might sound like an odd claim. Most conversations about AI are filled with buzzwords like machine learning, prompt engineering, AGI, large language models—terms you don’t often hear alongside plumbing and cable runs. But if you dig a little deeper, Huang’s point actually makes a lot of sense.

  • AI can’t fix your wiring: Hands-on, physical work—especially when it involves unpredictable environments—just isn’t something AI can easily automate. It’s not like coding or data entry. Every house is different. Every job requires problem solving. AI might help, but it can’t pull cables through a wall or install a fuse board for you.

  • More tech = more infrastructure: Smart homes, EVs, solar panels, IoT devices—all of these innovations still need physical installation and maintenance. That means more work for skilled tradespeople who understand both electricity and technology.

  • Labour value is shifting: As white-collar jobs become more automatable, skilled manual work may start to climb in both demand and respect. And if that happens, wages could follow.

Honestly, I think there’s something reassuring about that. In a world full of automation and digital chaos, becoming good at something practical feels like an anchor.

Why it matters to me

If you’ve read my earlier posts, you’ll know I’m currently training to become an electrician. I’ve worked in offices, I’ve done coding bootcamps, and I’ve explored other paths that were more digital or “future-proof.” But I got tired of sitting still, staring at screens. I wanted to do something real—something I could point to and say: I made that work.

The idea that a skilled trade might not only survive AI—but actually thrive because of it—adds even more weight to that decision. It’s not that I think I’ll be immune to disruption forever. But I do believe that learning a hands-on trade gives you resilience in a world that’s changing fast.

It’s funny—when I was younger, I was told that the future was digital, that everything would be online, that I’d need to work in tech to keep up. But maybe the most future-proof skill of all… is knowing how to wire up the future when it arrives.

Final thoughts

Will electricians and plumbers really "win" the AI race? I don’t think it’s about winning or losing. I think it’s about choosing a path that gives you freedom, value, and purpose. For me, that means working with my hands, solving real-world problems, and building something useful.

If the CEO of Nvidia is right, that’s just a bonus.

And if he’s wrong? I’ll still be learning a skill I respect—and that’s worth something too.
